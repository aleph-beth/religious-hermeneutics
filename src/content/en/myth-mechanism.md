# **Myth as Mechanism**

## Against the noise of information, toward an intelligence of deep structures

*Comparative mythology · Midrash · History of empires*

We live in an age convinced that more information produces more understanding. This conviction is false. What the world lacks is not data, but mechanism. And this mechanism we already have — it lies dormant in the myths we have stopped reading seriously.

---

# **I. Myth and religion — a fatal confusion**

There is a fundamental distinction, yet one constantly erased, between myth and religion. Religion, in its dominant forms, is literalist: it demands adherence to the narrative as historical fact, as first-degree truth. Myth, on the other hand, has never claimed literality. It is figurative writing, a compression of truths about the deep mechanics of humanity.

Religion sustains a belief. Myth tells a structure. It does not ask to be believed — it asks to be understood. The difference is abyssal. One closes, the other opens.

It is in this space that what I call midrash belongs — not as mere rabbinical commentary, but as a universal method: a perpetual rereading of foundational myths, in every era, to extract a new mechanism. Not exegesis, but an update. Myth as software that every generation must reinstall onto the reality of the world.

> *"Myth does not describe a past event. It describes a function that reincarnates indefinitely."*

---

# **II. Cain — the matrix of empire**

Consider the myth of Cain. After the murder of Abel the shepherd, this farmer condemned to wander founds the first city, naming it after his son. And his lineage produces exactly the three pillars of human civilization:

**Jabal**
Nomadic herding — Abel's legacy, absorbed by the one who killed him

**Jubal**
Music, the lyre, the flute — culture born from murder

**Tubal-Cain**
The smith of bronze and iron — technological warfare

The mechanism is staggering: the murderer of the innocent shepherd begets the entirety of human culture. Crime is not the opposite of the city. It is its matrix. Founding violence is not an accident of history — it is its condition.

The Islamic tradition adds a layer of rare density: a crow teaches Cain how to dig the earth to bury his brother. But for a farmer, to bury is also to plant. Guilt becomes seed. It grows into empire. The guilty act and the fertile act are, for the farmer, the same gesture.

---

# **III. Rome — the boar on the altar**

The same mechanism runs through Rome. Romulus kills Remus to trace the first furrow of the city. Both sons of Mars — god of war, certainly, but also god of agriculture. Destruction precedes foundation. One must die so the other can build.

But Rome goes further still in its symbolic construction. The *Legio X Fretensis* — the legion of Titus that destroyed Jerusalem in 70 CE — bore the boar as its emblem. This is no coincidence. In rabbinic literature, Rome is systematically symbolized by the pig — the *hazir*. The reason is already a mechanism: the pig displays cloven hooves — the outward sign of purity — but does not chew its cud — inner impurity. Rome is the power that displays law, justice, order, while being corrupt in its entrails. The appearance of legality, the reality of violence.

This identification of the pig with Rome then flows into Islamic tradition in a striking way. Among the signs of the end times, several hadiths describe the return of Jesus, who performs three acts: he breaks the cross, kills the Dajjal, and abolishes the pig. The construction is transparent: Jesus returns to symbolically undo what Rome did. The cross, instrument of Roman execution. The pig, emblem of the legion. Breaking the cross and killing the pig is the same act performed twice — undoing Pontius Pilate.

> *"The prohibition of pork is not a hygiene law. It is an encoded political act: refusing to commune with the empire that kills prophets."*

---

# **IV. Destroy to re-signify**

Antiochus Epiphanes, in 167 BCE, sacrifices a pig on the altar of the Temple — this is the abomination of desolation in the Book of Daniel. Jewish apocalyptic texts then see in Rome the repetition of Antiochus on a larger scale. The empire repeats itself with the same symbol, across centuries, because it fulfills the same function.

Rome builds the Colosseum on the symbolic ruins of the Temple of Jerusalem. The arena replaces the sanctuary. Spectacle supplants the sacred. The people prefer games to religion — what rabbinic and patristic commentators saw as an eschatological substitution: the Antichrist, at the end of times, will be the one who makes the divine playable, consumable, spectacular.

This mechanism did not die in antiquity. After Gaza, American teams proposed turning it into a riviera. Rome has always operated this way. So has the United States. Destroy to re-signify the space. Cain as the logical operator of imperial history. This is not an analogy. It is the mechanism at work, identical, across millennia.

---

# **V. Information is noise**

Let us return to the fundamental question. We live in an obsessively accumulative age: more data, more sources, more fact-checking, more analytical reports. And yet, understanding of the world does not progress. It regresses.

This is because information is noise. It accumulates facts without providing a structure to read them. It answers the question *what* without ever answering *why* or *how, again*. It documents the symptom, ignores the disease.

Myth, on the contrary — and with it philosophy — is a mechanism of cognitive compression. It encodes in a few images what a thousand analyses fail to say, because it touches structure, not events. A good myth is a predictive model. Cain does not describe a prehistoric fratricide. He describes Washington, Rome — and tomorrow.

Other logical operators confirm the method. The imperial eagle — Rome, the Third Reich, the United States — is the function of the empire that believes itself celestial. Babylon, in the Book of Revelation, designates Rome — and the Reformers would apply it to the papacy, Bob Marley to the colonial system. The name travels because it designates a function, not a city. Aaron's golden calf and the bronze bull in front of Wall Street are the same image: collective wealth is melted into an idol of power to be worshipped.

The world does not need more information to be understood. It needs better mechanisms.

And these mechanisms we already have. They lie dormant in the texts we have stopped rereading seriously — in the myths we have reduced to children's tales or beliefs for the naive.

To reread Cain is to understand empire. To reread the pig is to understand resistance. To reread Romulus is to understand foundation. Not as allegories — as logical operators of history that repeats itself, not by fatality, but because the same mechanisms, faced with the same conditions, produce the same results.

This is what midrash is. A reading that does not comment — it updates.
