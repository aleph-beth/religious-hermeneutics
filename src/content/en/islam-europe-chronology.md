## A Double Assault on Islamic Civilization

Between 1099 and 1291, the Islamic world faced a pincer movement from two directions: Crusaders from the West and Mongols from the East. At the same time, the Reconquista slowly dismantled al-Andalus, the most brilliant intellectual center of medieval Europe. These converging pressures destroyed the conditions that had made Islamic rationalism possible — and, paradoxically, scattered its seeds into Christian Europe.

This chronology traces the key events, placing **Cordoba**, **Jerusalem**, and **Baghdad** as the three poles of a single story.

## The Golden Age (8th–12th centuries)

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 711 | Muslim conquest of Iberia | Beginning of al-Andalus; centuries of convivencia |
| 762 | Foundation of Baghdad | Abbasid capital becomes the world's intellectual center |
| 813–833 | Caliph al-Ma'mun's reign | Foundation of the House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma); massive Greek-to-Arabic translation movement |
| 929 | Abd al-Rahman III declares the Caliphate of Cordoba | Cordoba rivals Baghdad: 400,000 volumes in its library, largest city in Europe |
| 1009–1031 | Fitna of al-Andalus | Collapse of the Caliphate of Cordoba into taifa kingdoms |
| 1085 | Fall of Toledo to Alfonso VI | **First transmission channel opens**: Gerard of Cremona translates 87 Arabic works into Latin |
| 1095 | Al-Ghazali writes *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* | Internal blow to Islamic rationalism (falsafa) — philosophy declared dangerous before the Crusades even begin |

## The Crusades and the Reconquista (1096–1291)

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1096–1099 | **First Crusade** | Conquest of Jerusalem (1099); massacre of Muslim and Jewish inhabitants |
| 1126 | Birth of Averroes (Ibn Rushd) in Cordoba | Last great Islamic rationalist philosopher |
| 1138 | Birth of Maimonides (Moshe ben Maimon) in Cordoba | Jewish philosopher who will bridge Aristotle to European thought |
| 1144 | Fall of Edessa | First Crusader state to fall; triggers Second Crusade |
| 1147–1149 | **Second Crusade** | Military failure for the Crusaders |
| 1148 | **Almohad conquest of al-Andalus** | Almohad fundamentalism drives out philosophers and Jews — Maimonides' family flees Cordoba |
| 1187 | **Saladin recaptures Jerusalem** | End of 88 years of Crusader rule; triggers Third Crusade |
| 1189–1192 | **Third Crusade** | Richard the Lionheart vs. Saladin; Jerusalem remains Muslim but coast recaptured |
| 1198 | Death of Averroes in Marrakech | His works, rejected in the Islamic world, will be translated into Latin and reshape European universities |
| 1202–1204 | **Fourth Crusade** | Sack of Constantinople instead of reaching the Holy Land — Crusades lose moral legitimacy |
| 1204 | Death of Maimonides in Cairo | His *Guide for the Perplexed* will travel through Provence, Italy, and eventually reach Spinoza in Amsterdam |
| ~1220 | Michael Scot translates Averroes' commentaries on Aristotle | **Latin Averroism begins**: Aristotle enters European universities through Arabic commentary |
| 1229 | **Sixth Crusade** (Frederick II) | Jerusalem recovered by negotiation, not warfare — brief interlude |
| 1236 | **Fall of Cordoba to Ferdinand III** | End of Islamic intellectual life in Andalusia; scholars scatter across the Mediterranean |
| 1244 | Jerusalem definitively lost by Crusaders | Khwarazmian mercenaries capture the city |
| 1248 | **Seventh Crusade** (Louis IX) | French king captured in Egypt; catastrophic defeat |
| 1250 | **Mamluk coup in Egypt** | Slave soldiers seize power — they will save Islam militarily |
| 1255 | **University of Paris mandates Aristotle** | The curriculum now requires texts that arrived via Arabic translation from Cordoba and Toledo |
| 1270 | **Eighth Crusade** (Louis IX) | Louis IX dies in Tunis; last major Western Crusade |
| 1270 | Bishop Tempier condemns Latin Averroism in Paris | Paradox: the condemnation proves Averroes' massive influence on Christian thought |
| ~1280–1290 | Composition of the Zohar in Castile | Third response to the faith-reason crisis, alongside Averroes (rationalism) and Maimonides (synthesis): Jewish mysticism |
| 1291 | **Fall of Acre** | End of the Crusader states; Mamluks complete the expulsion under al-Ashraf Khalil |

## The Mongol Storm (1206–1260)

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1206 | Temujin proclaimed Genghis Khan | Unification of Mongol tribes; beginning of the largest land empire in history |
| 1219–1221 | Mongol invasion of Khwarezm | Destruction of Bukhara, Samarkand, Merv — intellectual centers of eastern Islam |
| 1227 | Death of Genghis Khan | Empire divided among his sons; expansion continues |
| 1243 | Battle of Kose Dag | Mongols defeat the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum (Anatolia) |
| 1256 | Hulagu Khan destroys the Assassins (Alamut) | Mongol campaign against the Islamic heartland begins |
| **1258** | **Fall of Baghdad to Hulagu Khan** | **Catastrophe**: Caliph al-Musta'sim trampled to death; 200,000–1,000,000 killed; House of Wisdom destroyed; books thrown into the Tigris, said to have turned the river black with ink. End of the Abbasid Caliphate. End of Islam's Golden Age. |
| 1259 | Hulagu builds Maragha Observatory | Nuance: Nasir al-Din al-Tusi works under Mongol patronage — destruction is not total |
| **1260** | **Battle of Ain Jalut** | **Turning point**: Mamluk slaves defeat the "invincible" Mongols at Goliath's Spring. Generals Qutuz and Baybars (both Turkish kiptchak slaves) save Egypt, the holy cities, and Islam itself. Kitbuqa, the Mongol commander (a Nestorian Christian), is killed. |
| 1260 | Baybars assassinates Qutuz | Two months after Ain Jalut; Baybars becomes Sultan — begins systematic expulsion of the Crusaders |

| Date | Event | Significance |

## The Bifurcation: What Was Saved, What Was Lost

The Mamluks saved Islam's physical existence, its holy places, and its institutional continuity. But the price was the triumph of military orthodoxy over philosophical inquiry.

**What was saved**: territorial integrity, Cairo as cultural capital, jurisprudence (fiqh), Ash'arite theology, Sufism, historiography.

**What was not saved**: Averroesian falsafa, Mu'tazilite rationalism, intellectual pluralism, the chain from Aristotle to modernity.

### The Cordoba Paradox

Cordoba's destruction was fertile. Its fragments — carried by translators in Toledo, Jewish scholars through Provence and Italy, Kabbalists to Safed, scientists via Sicily — seeded the European Renaissance and Enlightenment.

Baghdad's salvation was sterile. The Mamluks, forged in existential war, built a military state that patronized architecture and law but not speculative philosophy.

**Destroyed Cordoba produced Spinoza. Saved Baghdad did not.**

The death of Islamic rationalism was not inevitable. It was the contingent result of:
- The Mu'tazilite defeat (10th century)
- Al-Ghazali's anti-philosophical turn (1095)
- The Crusades' militarization of Islamic society
- The Mongol destruction of Baghdad (1258)
- The absence of the printing press in the Islamic world
- Ottoman imperial centralization

The intellectual lineage that began with Aristotle, passed through Averroes in Cordoba and Maimonides in Cairo, and ended with Spinoza in Amsterdam, is the story of how knowledge survives through diaspora — how *death becomes seed*.
